Consultant Neurologist - Diagnosis (1)

A radiologically indiscernible right-sided brainstem stroke within the same vertebro-basilar arterial territory as the confirmed right cerebellar infarct, producing central post-stroke pain. Given the acute onset after the second AstraZeneca COVID-19 vaccine and the exhaustive negative cardiological, vascular, autoimmune, and infectious work-up, a vaccine-related thromboembolic mechanism is considered highly likely..

Planned / Ongoing Actions

  • Referral to Neuropsychiatry for pain management.
  • Open referral for second opinion with specialist in vaccine-related neurological injury.

Symptom Overview

Summary of symptom patterns and functional impact below.

Category Description Functional Impact
Throat & Facial Tension Left-sided throat and facial tightness, intermittent jaw and ear pressure, voice fatigue, and a persistent phlegm sensation (possible vagus nerve involvement). Voice tires with extended conversation or reading aloud; meals and hydration are slower on high-symptom days; public speaking or long calls increase strain and recovery time.
Leg, Foot & Groin Left calf heaviness, foot instability on uneven ground, intermittent tingling or numbness, groin and testicular pain, and pelvic floor tension (possible L4–S1 and pudendal/genitofemoral nerve involvement). Walking distance and pace reduced during flares; balance and single-leg stance feel less secure on the left; sitting tolerance affected by pelvic discomfort; sexual comfort and function fluctuate.
Upper Limb & Hand Left dorsal hand and wrist tingling with intermittent burning and pain that localises to the middle finger. Occasional wrist discomfort and reduced dexterity during flares. Typing, gripping, utensil use, and other fine motor tasks are slower and fatigue quickly; handwriting and precision decrease during symptom peaks.
Occipital & Scalp Patchy hair loss and regrowth within the occipital and posterior parietal scalp regions, correlating with greater, lesser, and third occipital nerve pathways (C2–C3). Features of occipital neuralgia with scalp allodynia. Full mapping and images are available in Occipital Scalp Changes - Nerve Path Mapping. Fluctuating scalp sensitivity and pain may coincide with hair density changes; prolonged pressure on the back of the head or temperature shifts can increase discomfort; tension or paresthesia may extend into the neck or upper trapezius.
Sensory & Pain Occipital area pain and pressure, intermittent facial and scalp paresthesia on the left, and occasional light-touch or temperature asymmetry. Central neuropathic pain overlay with peripheral triggers. Sustained screen time, bright lighting, or head movements increase occipital pain; sleep can be disrupted, reducing next-day stamina; requires pacing and heat-based relief.
Trigeminal-Occipital Convergence Persistent left-sided eyebrow-to-scalp pain radiating from the occipital region across the temple and into the cheek, with facial fullness and tingling. Distribution aligns with ophthalmic (V1) and maxillary (V2) trigeminal branches with possible occipital nerve interplay. Full Neuropathic Pain and Functional Impact Discomfort behind the eye and across the brow; bending forward can accentuate facial heaviness; contributes to sensory overload, light sensitivity, and fatigue on high-symptom days.
Visual & Vestibular Intermittent disturbance in the left visual field, eye strain, and dizziness with rapid head turns. Motion and visually busy environments increase symptoms. Reading and multitasking are slower; prefers larger fonts and reduced motion; needs regular breaks and steady visual anchors for balance.
Cognitive & Energy Mental fatigue, word-finding lapses, slower processing under cognitive load, and reduced stress tolerance, especially during flares. Complex tasks benefit from chunking and written checklists; requires recovery time after demanding activity; decision quality drops when multitasking or overtired.
Autonomic & General Fatigue disproportionate to effort, heart-in-throat sensations, and temperature intolerance during flares. Heightened reactivity to stress. Benefits from calm start-up to demanding interactions, breathing resets, gentle pacing, and consistent daily rhythm; overstimulation prolongs recovery.
