Diagnostic Hypotheses

Initial Hypothesis (July 2025)

Early imaging and clinical correlation suggested a right cerebellar infarct of uncertain cause. CT angiogram of the extracranial carotid/vertebral circulation and a bubble echocardiogram did not identify an embolic source. Lumbar puncture showed no oligoclonal bands or CSF inflammatory markers.

A subtle C5/6 cervical cord signal change on the 1.5 T study was interpreted as possible residual from prior myelitis, pending further correlation. Subsequent opinions queried whether this represented true cord pathology or artefact/resolving change.

Current Working Hypothesis (September–November 2025)

On 3 T review there is no demonstrable spinal cord signal change, making an active myelitis explanation less tenable. The evolving picture favors a small, radiologically indiscernible right-sided brainstem stroke in the same arterial territory as the cerebellar infarct as the most plausible unifying explanation for the persistent left-sided sensory symptoms and occipital pain.

Vascular work-up to date remains negative for an embolic source. Given the acute onset after the second AstraZeneca COVID-19 vaccine and the imaging correlation, the consultant considers a vaccine-related thromboembolic mechanism more likely than alternative explanations such as smoking history.

Central post-stroke pain is considered a leading component of the symptom profile.

MDT review scheduled: Cromwell Hospital, 5 November 2025, to correlate brainstem–cerebellar findings and agree further imaging or management.

Planned / Ongoing Actions

  • MDT review of brainstem and cerebellar findings on 5 November 2025, with cross-sequence correlation and prior study comparison.
  • Consider targeted brainstem MRI protocol if ambiguity persists after MDT discussion.
  • Trial of Carbamazepine (Tegretol) initiated: 100 mg once daily for 2 weeks, then 100 mg twice daily, to assess neuropathic modulation prior to MDT feedback.

Mast Cell Activation Differential & Further Investigations Requested

Following comprehensive autoimmune, infectious, and immunoglobulin screening, all of which returned negative or within reference range, a mast cell activation–mediated process remains a plausible untested contributor to the multisystem symptoms observed (neuropathic pain, pruritus, flushing, and autonomic instability).

Given the pattern of allergic-type reactions to multiple medications and autonomic dysregulation despite normal ANA, ENA, ANCA, complement, and immunoglobulin profiles, it is reasonable to evaluate for Mast Cell Activation Syndrome (MCAS) as part of the ongoing differential.

Recommended Investigations

Test Purpose Specimen
Serum Tryptase Baseline and repeat during a flare to detect mast cell mediator release. Blood (serum)
24-hr Urinary N-Methylhistamine Measures histamine metabolite to assess systemic mast-cell activity. Urine (24-hour collection)
24-hr Urinary Prostaglandin D₂ & 11-β-PGF₂α Detects mast-cell–derived prostanoid release contributing to inflammation or pain. Urine (24-hour collection)
24-hr Urinary Leukotriene E₄ Assesses eicosanoid pathway activation in hypersensitivity or MCAS. Urine (24-hour collection)

Clinical Rationale

Mast cell activation may bridge immunological and neurological mechanisms, aligning with the presentation of fluctuating neuropathic pain, pruritus, autonomic instability, drug hypersensitivity, and inflammatory reactivity. These investigations would clarify whether mast-cell mediator release underlies, amplifies, or mimics autoimmune or neuro-inflammatory processes.
