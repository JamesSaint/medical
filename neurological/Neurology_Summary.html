Diagnostic Hypotheses

Initial Hypothesis (July 2025)

Early imaging and clinical correlation suggested a right cerebellar infarct of uncertain cause. CT angiogram of the extracranial carotid/vertebral circulation and a bubble echocardiogram did not identify an embolic source. Lumbar puncture showed no oligoclonal bands or CSF inflammatory markers.

A subtle C5/6 cervical cord signal change on the 1.5 T study was interpreted as possible residual from prior myelitis, pending further correlation. Subsequent opinions have queried whether this represented true cord pathology or artefact/resolving change.

Current Working Hypothesis (September–November 2025)

The evolving picture favors a small right-sided brainstem or cerebellar ischemic event as the most plausible unifying explanation for the persistent left-sided sensory symptoms and occipital pain, with central post-stroke pain considered in the differential. On 3 T review, no robust demyelinating process has been demonstrated; the earlier cervical finding is considered equivocal.

A subtle brainstem T2 focus has been flagged for MDT radiology correlation to exclude artefact and to assess concordance with clinical pathways (with targeted brainstem protocol/DWI-ADC/FLAIR comparison if indicated). Vascular work-up to date remains negative for embolic source.

Temporal proximity to the AstraZeneca COVID-19 vaccination has been noted in the history; this is recorded as temporal correlation only, without inference of causality pending specialist review.

Planned / Ongoing Actions

  • MDT review of brainstem/cerebellar findings with correlation across sequences and prior studies.
  • Consider targeted brainstem MRI protocol if ambiguity persists (thin axial, optimized DWI/ADC, SWI as needed).
  • Symptom-led management pathway acknowledging possible central post-stroke pain.

Symptom Overview

Summary of symptom patterns and functional impact below.

Category Description Functional Impact
Throat & Facial Tension Left-sided throat and facial tightness, intermittent jaw and ear pressure, voice fatigue, and a persistent phlegm sensation (possible vagus nerve involvement). Voice tires with extended conversation or reading aloud; meals and hydration are slower on high-symptom days; public speaking or long calls increase strain and recovery time.
Leg, Foot & Groin Left calf heaviness, foot instability on uneven ground, intermittent tingling or numbness, groin and testicular pain, and pelvic floor tension (possible L4–S1 and pudendal/genitofemoral nerve involvement). Walking distance and pace reduced during flares; balance and single-leg stance feel less secure on the left; sitting tolerance affected by pelvic discomfort; sexual comfort and function fluctuate.
Sensory & Pain Occipital area pain and pressure, intermittent facial and scalp paresthesia (especially on the left), and occasional light-touch or temperature asymmetry. Sustained screen time, bright lighting, or head movements increase occipital pain; sleep can be disrupted, reducing next-day stamina.
Visual & Vestibular Intermittent disturbance in the left visual field, eye strain, and dizziness with rapid head turns. Reading small text and multitasking are slower; visually busy environments increase fatigue; prefers larger fonts, reduced motion, and paced gaze changes.
Cognitive & Energy Mental fatigue, word-finding lapses, slower processing under cognitive load, and reduced stress tolerance. Complex tasks benefit from chunking and written checklists; requires recovery time after demanding activity; decision quality drops when multitasking or overtired.
Autonomic & General Occasional “heart-in-throat” sensations, stress reactivity, and temperature intolerance during flares. Requires calm start-up for demanding interactions; benefits from breathing resets, gentle pacing, and consistent daily rhythm.
