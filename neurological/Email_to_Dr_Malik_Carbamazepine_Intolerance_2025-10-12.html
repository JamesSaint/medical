Urgent Review – Carbamazepine Intolerance and Drug Sensitivity Pattern

Dear Dr Malik,

I wanted to document and formally report a clear adverse reaction to carbamazepine. After two 100 mg doses, I developed systemic itching (scalp, underarms, chest, groin), autonomic instability, blurred vision, and no pain relief.

This mirrors my previous experiences with duloxetine, gabapentin, pregabalin, and amitriptyline—each producing comparable autonomic and histaminic reactions for which I was often advised to take antihistamines such as Phenergan or Chlorphenamine, with only very marginal benefit. All of these medications have been tracked for scientific integrity and documentation purposes but consistently demonstrate physiological intolerance rather than non-compliance or psychological overlay.

On 11 Oct 2025, around 1 p.m., approximately 22 hours after my first dose, I experienced a severe autonomic episode: dizziness, fluctuating pulse (111 → 85 → 110 bpm), chest and left-arm tightness, facial tingling, and a constricted sensation at the back of my head and spine.

On 12 Oct 2025, I awoke in the early hours with intense generalised pruritus. I contacted 111; an ambulance attended after about 2.5 hours. I had already taken chlorphenamine and cooled myself outside, which eased symptoms. Paramedics found stable vitals and a normal ECG (HR 100 bpm, PR 126 ms, QRS 91 ms, QTc 425 ms) and advised GP or neurology follow-up.

Given this reproducible pattern of drug intolerance across several neuro-active classes, I believe my system is reacting through an autonomic and mast-cell mechanism, likely exacerbated by post-vaccine neuro-inflammatory sensitivity.

I am requesting:

  1. Carbamazepine to be listed as an intolerant or adverse reaction in my record.
  2. Consideration of mast-cell activation testing (tryptase, histamine, DAO).
  3. Pharmacogenomic profiling (CYP2D6 and CYP3A4) prior to any further prescriptions.
  4. Discussion of Nabiximols (THC–CBD oromucosal spray) as the most viable treatment path. Given that these drug sensitivities have followed the AstraZeneca vaccination, I believe NHS support for this option is appropriate.

To date I have demonstrated clear intolerance to the antidepressant and anticonvulsant medications listed above. Their reproducible adverse pattern supports a biological mechanism rather than a psychosomatic one. I am documenting this for ongoing transparency and to guide a safe, evidence-based treatment plan.

Given this pattern, I would like to ask whether a monitored trial of Nabiximols might now be clinically appropriate. The balanced THC–CBD formulation is relevant to central post-stroke or brainstem-related neuropathic pain, acting through both nociceptive modulation and neuro-inflammatory pathways.

While its UK licence currently covers multiple-sclerosis-related spasticity, emerging evidence supports cautious off-label use in other central neuropathic pain syndromes. I would value your guidance on whether this could be incorporated within my current management plan and considered by the multidisciplinary team.

If you feel this justified, I would greatly appreciate your advice on:

  • whether a short monitored trial could be arranged under your supervision or via a pain specialist in your network;
  • any formal criteria, consent, or funding routes required (including AXA or NHS exceptional-case pathways); and
  • appropriate starting and titration parameters.

I understand and support the need for evidence-based prescribing. My intention is to find a pharmacological option that provides measurable relief and functional stability without further triggering the autonomic or mast-cell reactions documented with previous agents.

I am not refusing treatment; I am asking for a path that my body can tolerate. Please let me know how best to proceed and whether additional investigations can be arranged.

Kind regards,
James Saint

Mast Cell Activation Differential & Further Investigations Requested

Following comprehensive autoimmune, infectious, and immunoglobulin screening, all of which returned negative or within reference range, a mast cell activation–mediated process remains a plausible untested contributor to the multisystem symptoms observed (neuropathic pain, pruritus, flushing, and autonomic instability).

Given the pattern of allergic-type reactions to multiple medications and autonomic dysregulation despite normal ANA, ENA, ANCA, complement, and immunoglobulin profiles, it is reasonable to evaluate for Mast Cell Activation Syndrome (MCAS) as part of the ongoing differential.

Clinical Rationale

Mast cell activation may bridge immunological and neurological mechanisms, aligning with the presentation of fluctuating neuropathic pain, pruritus, autonomic instability, drug hypersensitivity, and inflammatory reactivity. These investigations would clarify whether mast-cell mediator release underlies, amplifies, or mimics autoimmune or neuro-inflammatory processes.
