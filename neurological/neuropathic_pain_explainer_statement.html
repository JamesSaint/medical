Neuropathic Pain Explainer and Functional Impact

Audience: clinicians, legal teams, family and friends. Written to be clear, factual, and human.

Introduction

I live with severe chronic neuropathic pain following a vaccine injury. The pain focuses on the left side of my head and neck, consistent with occipital neuralgia and trigeminal neuralgia. Sensations are burning, stabbing, electric, and pressure-like. There is no true pain-free window.

Pain Severity Scale

On a 0 to 10 scale where 0 is no pain and 10 is the worst imaginable, my typical levels are:

Baseline and flare levels with plain language anchors.
Level Anchor Description My Reality
0 to 3 Mild. Noticeable but not limiting. Rare. I do not experience this range.
4 to 6 Moderate. Work and conversation require effort. Uncommon. Only with short-lived relief.
7 to 8 Severe. Function is limited. Concentration and sleep are disrupted. My baseline most days. Constant pain at this level.
9 Excruciating. Speech, movement, and thinking are impaired. Frequent flares. Triggered by small movements or sensory input. Can last minutes to hours.
10 Unbearable. Emergency level. Approached during worst spikes.

Examples of everyday triggers: turning my head, brushing hair, chewing, speaking, bright light, loud sound, cold air on the scalp or face.

What the Pain Feels Like

  • Electric shock jolts in the scalp, eye, jaw, and temple on the left side.
  • Burning line of pain along the path of the occipital nerves at the back of the head and neck.
  • Stabbing or ice pick sensations behind the left eye and in the cheek.
  • Pressure, swelling, and hypersensitivity to touch on the scalp and face.

Cognitive and Functional Impact

  • Cognitive load: persistent pain signals reduce available attention and working memory. Reading, writing, and decision making slow down.
  • Word finding and speech: during flares I struggle to find words and to speak clearly.
  • Sensory intolerance: light, sound, and motion can intensify pain. I often need a dark, quiet room.
  • Fatigue: sleep is disrupted and shallow. The combination of pain and poor sleep produces daytime exhaustion.
  • Activities of daily living: driving, cooking, social contact, and basic tasks often need to be shortened, simplified, or postponed.
  • Reliability: symptoms vary hour by hour. I can appear capable one moment and then be unable to function the next.

Duration and Psychological Toll

  • Chronic course: this has persisted for an extended period without meaningful remission.
  • Emotional impact: living at a constant 7 to 8 with spikes to 9 narrows life to endurance. Hope and motivation fluctuate.
  • Isolation: unpredictability and sensory limits reduce social contact and community.
  • Trust: prolonged invalidation or minimisation increases distress. Being believed and taken seriously is stabilising.

Common Triggers and Care Notes

  • Head and neck movement, especially rotation and extension.
  • Touch, grooming, shaving, or wearing tight hats or glasses on the affected side.
  • Cold wind or sudden temperature changes on the face or scalp.
  • Loud environments, bright or flickering light.
  • Prolonged screen use and cognitive overload.

Care interactions are safest when calm, unhurried, and collaborative. Rapid or forceful examination of the scalp, neck, or face can provoke severe flares.

Practical Accommodations That Help

  • Quiet, low light settings for appointments or conversations.
  • Allowing extra time, breaks, and written summaries after discussions.
  • Flexible scheduling with the option to reschedule if a flare is severe.
  • Permission to lie down, close eyes, or reduce input without penalty.
  • Gentle physical examination, avoiding pressure on the occipital region.

For Clinicians

  • Severe neuropathic pain with occipital and trigeminal neuralgia features, left dominant.
  • Baseline 7 to 8, frequent spikes to 9. Pain and sensory reactivity significantly impair function.
  • Cognitive fog and dysfluency are pain related, not a lack of effort.
  • Plan with sensory limits in mind. Provide clear written plans and allow paced follow up.

For Legal Teams

  • Constant severe pain with documented neurological features limits reliability, employability, and daily function.
  • Symptoms have persisted for a prolonged period and are not fully responsive to standard therapies tried to date.
  • The condition imposes significant care needs, lifestyle restrictions, and psychological burden.

For Family and Friends

  • Believe me when I say the pain is severe even if I look calm. I am managing input to survive the day.
  • Short, simple check ins help. Noise, bright light, and long calls can make things worse.
  • Practical help is valuable. Quiet company, meals, lifts, or help with tasks reduce strain.
  • Plans may change at short notice due to flares. It is not a lack of care. It is the reality of this condition.

Summary

This is a daily, high level neurological pain state that restricts thinking, energy, and function. Clear communication, sensory aware care, and flexible planning make a meaningful difference to my safety and quality of life.
