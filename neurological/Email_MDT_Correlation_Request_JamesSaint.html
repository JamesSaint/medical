MDT Correlation Request

To: Dr Malik and MDT colleagues
Subject: Correlation request — subtle brainstem T2 observation and clinical picture

Dear Dr Malik and MDT,

Thank you for reviewing my case. I’m not a neurologist or radiologist; I’m a patient with a background in AI and pattern recognition. My intention here is not to diagnose, but to clearly present what I noticed on the axial T2 images and ask for your expert correlation.

Context

  • History includes a right cerebral infarct previously discussed.
  • Ongoing left‑sided sensory symptoms and pain, with occipital involvement.
  • Working hypothesis (per recent discussion): brainstem process could better account for the clinical picture than spinal cord involvement.

Observation (Axial T2)

On review of the axial T2 series, I noted a subtle focus of increased signal within the brainstem region on one slice. I fully appreciate this could be artefact, normal variation, or partial‑volume effect. I’m therefore requesting expert eyes on whether this finding is consistent with the evolving hypothesis.

Why I believe it merits correlation

  • The focal signal appears qualitatively brighter than adjacent parenchyma on that slice, without obvious mass effect.
  • If real, its location could plausibly intersect pathways relevant to the left‑sided symptoms (to be confirmed by you).
  • This aligns with the current clinical hypothesis that a small brainstem event could explain the presentation.

Important: I recognise common pitfalls in this region (pulsation, flow, susceptibility, and partial volume). I’m explicitly asking for radiology correlation to confirm or exclude this as an artefact.

Specific requests to the MDT

  1. Please verify whether the subtle T2 focus seen in Figure 2 represents a true parenchymal signal change versus artefact.
  2. If helpful, correlate with DWI/ADC and FLAIR on the same level, and consider comparison with any prior brain MRIs for stability or evolution.
  3. Advise on whether a targeted brainstem protocol (thin‑slice axial, reduced EPI distortion DWI, and SWI as indicated) would be informative if ambiguity remains.
  4. Comment on concordance (or not) with the left‑sided symptoms and the previously noted right cerebral infarct.

Closing

I’m offering these observations in the spirit of collaboration. I’d appreciate your view on whether this subtle finding is consistent with the brainstem hypothesis, or more likely an artefact or normal variation. Thank you for your care and expertise.

With appreciation,
James Saint
